Aria

Feb. - May 2019 (3 months) | UX & UI Design

Tools: Adobe Illustrator, InDesign, XD

About the project

Aria is a concert streaming app that creates a gateway into the music experience for those who can't afford to attend live concerts.

    Aria: Get more from life with music.

The problem

Students in GRC 200: Design Thinking & Methodologies were tasked to build a hypothetical company with new visual identities based on an existing brand.

Aria was derived from the brand Netflix, Inc., a streaming service that allows people to watch a wide variety of TV shows, movies, and documentaries.

Users & audience

Concerts and festivals can be pricey. Not everyone can afford to buy a ticket and travel around to see their favorite bands and/or artists live. The target audience of this product is curated specifically towards teenagers and young adults.

The process

Brainstorming & Research

I started out the project by conducting an initial user research to find out what young adults are interested in, in terms of music. I had people write on sticky notes to achieve this research.

With this in place, I had a better understanding about the needs of the target audience and how it can be created specifically towards a certain age group. I knew that the product had to be:

  • Adventurous
  • Youthful
  • Energetic
  • Upbeat

    • Logos

    Since most users are young adults, I wanted the logo to reflect the target audience. I made the logo simple yet still be recognizable. The colors reflected the energetic and upbeat stage lights that would shine throughout the event.

    Stationary Identity

    The stationary identity revolves around the color scheme of the logo. The three colored bars shown on the stationary represents what a headphone jack would look like.

    Wireframes

    Visual Design

    Using Adobe Illustrator and Adobe XD, I was able to create the visual design of what the app would hypothetically look like. Some design elements derived from Youtube and Spotify features.

    Main takeaways

    Throughout this project, I learned:

  • Not only is design important, but the user experience is crucial. Make elements simple and easy enough for users to navigate.
  • Being consistent in design ties everything together to make it a whole.

    • Lucky Find

    Sept. 2017 - May 2018 (6 months) | Web Design & Development | Senior Capstone

    About the project

    Lucky Find revolves around the area of traveling. Meeting people from other places show that your world view isn't the same as everyone else's. Being exposed to new places, people and cultures, travelers will develop a wide world view. The shared experience of travel brings people together and we wanted to help travelers find the best attractions that locals love.

      The name Lucky Find symbolizes the rarity and chance encounter of finding interesting attractions outside of the regular tourist spot.

    The problem

    Living in Las Vegas, many tourists usually only visit places such as the Strip, when in reality, there are many more amazing sights to explore in the city.

    Team & My Role

    Working in a group of two, my partner and I split up most of the work. My partner created the written content while I coded and developed the website. We both did our fair share of designing everything from the wireframe to the final website.

    The Process

    Roadmap

    We planned to start covering attractions locally in Las Vegas and eventually expand to different cities and states in the United States.

    Wireframe

    Mockup

    Locations

    We divided Las Vegas by its communities to make it easier for users to filter attractions by specific areas. Within each community, we filtered out entertainments that were both paid and free sites. Lastly, we included restaurants that are local to support local businesses in our community.

    With different competitors that showcase attractions, we believe that we are unique in our own way because we tend to stay away from showing tourist sites. Additionally, all attractions are handpicked by locals in Las Vegas who are helping to support local businesses.

    Main Takeaways

    The biggest barrier that my team faced was being able to narrow down the attractions that were worth showcasing. As we avoid sharing tourist spots, we had to do extra research to find which attractions would fit our criteria of being a hole-in-the-wall site.

    Rockin' Robots

    Feb. - March 2017 (1 month) | Web Design & Development | Team Project

    Languages: SQL & PHP

    About the project

    Rockin' Robots features different kinds of robots, from toy robots to service robots.

    The challenge

    With an introduction to data, databases, SQL, and the basics of PHP, students working in pairs were tasked to create a database and an online store presence for an industry.

    The goals

    Choosing robots as our industry, we knew that our website had to reflect the products that we were trying to showcase. We wanted our website to be:

  • New and unique
  • Clean and simple
  • User friendly


    • The process

    Wireframe

    Our wireframe mimics what a typical online store would look like.

    Mockups

    Front page of the website with a hero image and text inviting audience to check out the products.

    Left: Products page with all the robots. Filters located on the left and top of the page.

    Right: Specific product page. Showcases the image, name, price, and description of the robot. Similar items recommended from the store are located below the product.

    Code & database

    We created a table which included the ID number, name, description, type, price, and image of each item in the online store.

    The results

    We got rid of the navigation bar on the bottom to avoid the website looking cluttered. We also added a button underneath the large heading on the hero image that leads to the shop page.

    The shop page showcases the product with a description of it when the customer hovers over the image.

    Main takeaways

    Throughout this project, I've learned that there is a lot that goes into collecting inventory of items. Not only do you need the name and price of the object, the type, description, and item numbers are also crucial in creating a successful online store.
